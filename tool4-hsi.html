Recovery outcomes are influenced by more than medical treatment alone. Internal stability and environmental pressures interact to either support or destabilize recovery.

The Holistic Stability Index™ evaluates these structural forces to help identify hidden pressures affecting recovery progress and case outcomes.

The Holistic Stability Index™ is most meaningful when scored from direct client input. Best practice is a structured phone call with the client, walking through each item together.

  1. Clinical accuracy. Self-report on psychological, psychosocial, and destabilizer items reflects what the client is actually experiencing — not what the case file happens to document.
  2. Evidentiary record. A dated, structured client-reported assessment becomes a contemporaneous record of pain, functional limitation, emotional impact, and treatment burden. That record has independent value in negotiations, mediation, and demand packages, and a series of HSI runs over the life of a case creates a trajectory that is difficult to dispute.
  3. Documentation defensibility. Record the date of the client interaction and document that responses were obtained directly from the client whenever possible. Direct client-reported responses strengthen the evidentiary value of this assessment.

Holistic Stability Index™ — Instructions

The Three-Axis Model

HSI measures stability across three axes:

  • Intrinsic (4Ps of Wellness): the person's own state across Maslow's hierarchy — Physical (P1), Psychological (P2), Psychosocial (P3), Professional (P4). The intrinsic score is the lowest P-domain score, because stability cannot exceed the weakest tier.
  • Extrinsic (Social Drivers of Health): environmental conditions acting on the person — housing, food, transportation, insurance, and six others. Scored as the average across the nine drivers.
  • Destabilizers (Pain, Depression, Anxiety): contextual modifiers that color how intrinsic and extrinsic scores should be read. Destabilizers do not change intrinsic or extrinsic scores.

Scoring Guidance

  • Scores are whole numbers, 1–5. A score of 5 indicates the strongest stability; a score of 1 indicates critical instability.
  • If more than one stage appears applicable, select the lowest stage that reflects the individual's current condition. The lowest domain score determines intrinsic stability.
  • Conduct the assessment through direct client contact when possible. Record the date of the client interaction and note that responses were obtained directly from the client. Attorney inference from case records is acceptable when client contact is unavailable, but the source of responses should always be noted.

What to Look For

  • Psychological (P2) scores at 1 or 2, or any Destabilizer at 1, warrant a welfare check or clinical referral within 24–48 hours.
  • Divergence between intrinsic and extrinsic scores (a gap of 2 or more points) signals meaningful clinical-legal patterns worth flagging in case strategy.
  • Re-run HSI at intervals appropriate to case acuity. The Recommended Next Actions block in each report shows the follow-up cadence for the current tier.
