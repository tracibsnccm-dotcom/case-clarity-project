Functional Capacity Index™ — Instructions

How FCI Works

FCI produces two outputs:

  • Functional Capacity Index (FCI): the minimum score across the four P-domains (Physical, Psychological, Psychosocial, Professional). FCI tells you where the client is functioning right now — the lowest domain score determines overall functional capacity, because stability cannot exceed the weakest area.
  • Functional Vulnerability Indicator (FVI): a categorical output (High, Moderate, or Low) computed from financial context (income, dependents, sole earner), pain level, and psychological stress. FVI tells you what external pressure is acting on the client's recovery trajectory.

FCI measures where the person is functioning. FVI measures what pressure is acting on their recovery. Both are reported separately so the attorney can read them together or apart.

Scoring Guidance

  • Scores are whole numbers, 1–5. A score of 5 indicates the most stable functioning; a score of 1 indicates critical instability.
  • If more than one level appears applicable, select the lowest level that reflects the client's current condition.
  • Pain and Psychological Stress ratings feed into the FVI calculation. They do not change the FCI score.
  • Score against the clinical picture available in case records, intake notes, and attorney-client communication.

What to Look For

  • Physical (P1) or Psychological (P2) at SEVERE or CRITICAL, or Pain or Psychological Stress at CRITICAL, warrant attention to whether current clinical support and treatment access are adequate.
  • FCI and FVI divergence reveals meaningful patterns: high FCI with high FVI suggests stable-but-vulnerable (watch for destabilization under pressure), while low FCI with low FVI suggests functional impairment from the injury itself rather than from external pressure.
  • Re-run FCI to document how functional capacity changes over the life of the case. Serial assessments create a trajectory that is more valuable than a single snapshot.
