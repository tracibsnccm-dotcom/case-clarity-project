Before You Begin — Understanding the C.A.R.E. Access Friction Score™

Twenty-five questions across five domains. The report highlights systemic barriers to access and continuity—not individual blame. Goal: clarity, not speed.

Disruptions often come from payer, authorization, logistics, and documentation friction—not from a single person. This tool helps you spot those barriers early for monitoring and file documentation.

  • Answer from the case environment as it stands today.
  • Take enough time to be accurate; rushed answers skew the picture.
  • Reconcile C.A.R.E. (Clinical Analysis & Recovery Entitlements) is the broader methodology; this score is one structured lens on access friction.

Each domain separates questions into two observation types. Voice / View — Client Experience captures what the client reports (difficulties they describe, barriers they encounter). Clinical / System Review captures what the record shows (timelines, documentation patterns, authorization sequences).

Both contribute to the score. The distinction helps clarify which barriers were reported and which were observed.

Each domain maps to common failure points in treatment access. The lowest domain level drives the overall friction level.

  • 1. Provider Access — availability, referrals, willingness to treat in litigation/lien contexts.
  • 2. Insurance & authorization — payer rules, UR, delays, denials.
  • 3. Transportation & travel — getting to appointments reliably.
  • 4. Administrative & documentation — IDs, records, billing, timeliness.
  • 5. Care coordination — communication across providers and the plan of care.

Functional stability is expressed in whole-number levels, not decimals—small fractional differences do not change the interpreted level. Capability at one level versus the next can differ materially; the assessment avoids false precision.

  • Higher levels are not always achievable (e.g., MMI below “full” function applies to environments too).
  • The aim is an accurate stability picture for decisions—not inflating the score.

Each domain uses its five answers only. Domain totals convert to a whole-number level by rounding down.

The overall Access Friction Score™ follows the lowest domain—the weakest barrier caps system stability.

Barriers can look like non-compliance or poor progress later if they stay invisible. Use this to monitor the file, tighten focus, and document systemic friction while treatment is ongoing. Reconcile C.A.R.E. is built to track and address these same issues across the life of a case.

The friction level provides the baseline monitoring cadence for this tool. The triggers below indicate circumstances that may justify an off-cycle re-run before the next scheduled review point.

Level-based review cadence:

Re-run this assessment when:

  • An authorization denial is received or the payer changes position
  • Transportation arrangement changes (provider change, loss of coverage, new geographic constraint)
  • The client reports a new access barrier not captured at last assessment
  • Treatment delay exceeds the expected interval between scheduled visits
  • A new specialist or referral is added to the care plan
  • A provider withdraws or refuses continued treatment
  • The case transitions between pre-litigation, litigation, and settlement phases
  • Case type or claim status changes materially (e.g., WC claim accepted or denied, PI subrogation added)

How to Use This Assessment

Two Observation Lenses

Each domain separates questions into two observation types so the assessment captures both what the client describes and what the record shows.

  • Voice / View — Client Experience: what the client reports about their own access to care (e.g., difficulty obtaining appointments, transportation challenges).
  • Clinical / System Review: observations drawn from the record itself (e.g., authorization timelines, documentation gaps, coordination patterns).

Both lenses contribute to the same score. The distinction helps the attorney understand which barriers were reported and which were observed.

What to Look For — by Section

Section 1 — Provider Access

Focus on whether the client is able to access appropriate providers within reasonable timeframes.

Pay close attention to:

  • Delays in obtaining appointments
  • Provider refusal due to litigation or payment issues
  • Limited specialist availability
  • Geographic barriers
  • Scheduling delays beyond expected intervals

Access barriers may delay diagnosis and treatment progression.

Section 2 — Insurance & Authorization

Focus on whether treatment approvals are occurring without unnecessary delay.

Pay close attention to:

  • Authorization delays
  • Denials for medical necessity
  • Missing appeals
  • Referral gaps
  • Repeated utilization review interruptions

Authorization failures may interrupt treatment continuity.

Section 3 — Transportation & Travel Access

Transportation reliability directly affects treatment attendance and continuity.

Pay close attention to:

  • Missed visits due to transportation failure
  • Long travel distances
  • Inconsistent ride availability
  • Appointment cancellations
  • Changes in transportation reliability

Transportation failures may result in treatment gaps that affect recovery and case credibility.

Section 4 — Administrative & Documentation Integrity

Focus on whether documentation supports medical necessity using clear and specific language.

Pay close attention to:

  • Vague clinical language
  • Missing measurable findings
  • Inconsistent diagnostic terms
  • Incomplete documentation
  • Administrative errors

Weak documentation may result in treatment denials and delays.

Section 5 — Care Coordination

Focus on whether treatment activities are progressing in a structured and coordinated manner.

Pay close attention to:

  • Referral delays
  • Communication gaps
  • Duplicated services
  • Conflicting treatment plans
  • Missing follow-through

Poor coordination may delay recovery progression.

Clinical-Legal Considerations

⚠ Litigation & Treatment Risk — Denials

When treatment is denied as "Not Medically Necessary," best-practice clinical-legal response includes prompt appeal of the denial.

Unchallenged denials may be interpreted as acceptance of the payor's treatment decision — in effect, aligning with the payor rather than the treating provider. Failure to appeal may create the appearance that the treatment was not necessary, because if it had been, the treating provider would have challenged the denial.

This pattern may weaken the medical foundation of the case and affect future treatment authorization. Response decisions remain at attorney discretion.

🚗 Transportation Verification — Best Practice

Best-practice clinical-legal standards recommend verifying transportation availability at least 48 hours before scheduled appointments. Advance verification reduces missed visits and preserves documentation of treatment attendance.

Verification practices remain at attorney discretion.

⚠ Litigation Risk — Transportation

Missed visits may be interpreted as treatment non-compliance. Transportation failures warrant early attention to prevent credibility challenges later in the case.

⚠ Care Coordination Advisory — PI vs Workers' Comp

In personal injury cases, coordination across providers may be limited.

In workers' compensation cases, assigned care managers represent the employer or payor and may influence care direction. Care progression should be monitored to ensure decisions support the client's recovery needs.
