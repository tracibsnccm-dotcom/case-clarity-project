Before You Begin — Understanding the C.A.R.E. Access Friction Score™

A structured assessment designed to identify systemic barriers that may disrupt treatment continuity, documentation integrity, and recovery progression in injury cases.

About This Assessment

Many treatment disruptions are not caused by the client or the provider alone, but by friction within the healthcare and insurance systems.

The purpose of this assessment is to help identify those barriers early so they can be addressed and documented appropriately, reducing the risk of treatment gaps that can ultimately undermine the strength of a case.

The C.A.R.E. Access Friction Score™ is part of the Reconcile C.A.R.E. (Clinical Analysis & Recovery Entitlements) methodology, developed by RN Care Managers to help attorneys identify and monitor systemic barriers that interfere with treatment continuity and recovery progression.

About the Assessment Process

This assessment is designed to provide a structured evaluation of potential barriers affecting treatment continuity in injury cases.

While the assessment can be completed in several minutes, it was not designed to be rushed. Thoughtful responses will produce a more accurate picture of where systemic barriers may be affecting recovery and documentation.

The goal is not speed, but clarity.

What This Assessment Evaluates

The C.A.R.E. Access Friction Score™ evaluates five domains that commonly interfere with treatment continuity and case stability.

1
Provider Access
Evaluates whether appropriate medical providers are realistically available to treat the injuries involved in the case, including specialist availability, referral access, and whether providers are willing to treat in litigation or lien-based environments.
2
Insurance & Authorization Barriers
Evaluates whether payer requirements, authorization delays, utilization review decisions, or administrative denials are preventing treatment from occurring when medically necessary.
3
Transportation & Travel Access
Evaluates whether the client can reliably attend appointments due to transportation availability, travel distance, mobility limitations, or logistical barriers.
4
Administrative & Documentation Integrity
Evaluates administrative issues that may interfere with treatment progression, including incorrect identifying information, documentation delays, incomplete records, or claim identification problems.
5
Care Coordination
Evaluates whether treating providers are communicating and coordinating effectively so referrals, treatment progression, and documentation remain consistent throughout the case.
How the C.A.R.E. Framework Interprets Scores

In economic contexts, the difference between $4.99 and $5.00 is essentially meaningless. One cent rarely determines whether someone buys an item. In terms of money, those two numbers are practically the same.

Human capability and functioning do not work that way.

When we evaluate human capability or system stability, for example, 4.99 and 5 are not the same state. A level 5 represents full stability. A level 4.99 may be close, but it is still not the same level of capability.

In the C.A.R.E. framework, everything behind the decimal point is simply noise. When we focus too heavily on decimals or percentages, we can lose sight of what the base number actually represents.

Whether a value is 4.00, 4.50, or 4.99, the functional state is still 4. That means the system is operating at roughly 80% capacity, not 5, which represents full stability.

Why This Distinction Matters
  • 1
    Capability Changes Between LevelsWhat a person or system can sustain at 80% capacity versus 100% capacity can be dramatically different.
  • 2
    Operating Above True Capacity Creates RiskAttempting to operate at a level the underlying conditions cannot support increases the risk of instability, reinjury, or system breakdown.
  • 3
    Inflated Scores Create False StabilityOverstating stability can create a false sense of progress. Clients, providers, and payors may begin to believe the situation is more stable than it actually is.

If a system is truly operating at a higher level of stability, the assessment results should demonstrate that. Advancing to the next level should occur because the underlying conditions support it — not because numbers were rounded to reach a higher score.

For this reason, the C.A.R.E. methodology evaluates stability using whole-number functional levels rather than decimals or percentages.

It is also important to recognize that reaching the highest level is not always possible. In clinical recovery, a patient may reach Maximum Medical Improvement below full function. The same principle applies when evaluating case environments: a system may stabilize below the maximum possible score.

The purpose of the assessment is not to inflate the score, but to accurately identify the system's true level of stability so informed decisions can be made.

Important Scoring Note

Each domain is scored independently based on the five responses within that section.

The domain total is converted to a whole-number functional level by rounding down.

The overall C.A.R.E. Access Friction Score™ is then determined by the lowest domain level, because system stability cannot exceed its weakest barrier.

Why This Matters to Attorneys

System barriers can delay treatment, interrupt referrals, complicate documentation, and create gaps that later appear to be non-compliance or poor recovery progression.

This assessment helps attorneys identify those barriers earlier so they can monitor the case more effectively, narrow their focus sooner, and address issues before they create larger treatment or documentation problems.

About Reconcile C.A.R.E.

The issues highlighted in this assessment are the very types of barriers Reconcile C.A.R.E. was designed to identify, monitor, and help resolve.

The C.A.R.E. methodology focuses on improving care coordination, identifying treatment barriers early, supporting documentation integrity, and helping attorneys maintain treatment continuity throughout the life of a case.
